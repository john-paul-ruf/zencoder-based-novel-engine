## Author Notes — Chapter 2

**Voice decisions:**
- Deep interiority remains the primary engine. Cole observes everyone through the lens of assessment — skill sets, psychological resilience, survival probability. This is consistent with Ch 1's "counting faces" mode but shifts from signing-room snapshot to sustained three-month study.
- Polk's freedom rhetoric rendered as overheard dialogue, filtered through Cole's reading of it as "damage, not ideology." Cole hears the wound underneath the performance. This establishes Polk's voice (loud, declarative, folksy-smart) while keeping Cole's analytical distance.
- Dana introduced through what she does rather than what she says: paper notebook, tiny handwriting, avoiding eye contact. The prose mirrors her self-effacement — she gets less space than Polk or Ren because she's designed not to take up space. This will pay off when her POV arrives in Ch 14.
- Haruki's medical questions ("How long without your medication? What happens when resupply stops?") delivered as indirect speech to keep the scene moving while establishing his quiet thoroughness.
- Joss's "panicking and preparing in roughly equal measure" — the two contradictory behaviors define his character: anxiety channeled into action, fear that becomes diligence. This sets up his arc from anxious kid to colony builder.
- Polk encounter reworked to show debt math on the napkin (not survival threshold), matching outline's "comparing charter escape vs. Earth debt obligations." Polk's "Built a whole business on numbers once" plants the co-op backstory without exposition.

**Structural choices:**
- Chapter moves from wide (ship atmosphere) to narrow (Ren meal) to wide (mess observations) to narrow (Polk encounter) to wide (briefing). Alternating focal distance keeps the transit chapter from feeling static.
- Data gaps in the surveys (line 9) plant a seed for Ch 6's ghost colony discovery without spoiling it. Cole notices missing data but doesn't know what was removed. The payoff is structural: when he finds Elena's logs, the reader retroactively understands what the gaps were hiding.
- Viability score scene: Cole has the number memorized (not looked up) — establishes his obsessive data retention as character trait. The Ren eye-contact moment does double duty: confirms their emerging alliance and shows they process information the same way.
- "Some information was only useful if the right person had it" — Cole's withholding of clause 9.4.2 from Polk is the chapter's key moral beat. He's already making survival decisions that require other people to stay ignorant. The "monster's work" line (ranking people by survival probability) is the self-aware version of the same instinct.
