## Characters

### Maeve Dónal
- **Role:** Co-protagonist
- **Age:** 38
- **Physical Description:** Tall, lean, long-fingered. Dark hair she keeps pulled back in a low knot that loosens through the day. A face that settles into stillness when she's not performing — angular, watchful, older than she is around the eyes. Hands always nicked or calloused from lighthouse work. Wears the same three jumpers in rotation. No jewelry except the wedding ring she turns but never looks at, until the day she stops wearing it (Chapter 14).
- **Speech Patterns:** Short, deflecting sentences. Answers questions with questions. Speaks more freely about objects and tasks than about herself. Her voice drops half a register when she's being dishonest. Uses silence as punctuation. Swears only when startled — sharp, single words.
- **Want:** To disappear — to live without being seen or known
- **Need:** To be seen fully and forgiven, starting with forgiving herself
- **Flaw/Wound:** Her marriage was suffocating. Aidan's death was a relief. That truth is eating her alive beneath a performance of widowhood she cannot sustain.
- **Key Detail:** She recognized Callum the moment he arrived. She followed his story in the press. She believed him when no one else did. This knowledge gives her power — and makes every gesture of intimacy a lie by omission.
- **Arc:** From performance → concealment → crisis → confession (to Callum, Ch 23) → confession to herself (Ch 25) → honest vulnerability.
- **First Appearance:** Chapter 1 (the-keeper)

### Callum Greig
- **Role:** Co-protagonist
- **Age:** 41
- **Physical Description:** Medium height, broad-shouldered but thinning — a man who used to take up more space. Sandy hair going grey at the temples. Wire-framed glasses he pushes up his nose when thinking. Clean-shaven when he arrives; grows a beard through the winter. Hands that move when he talks — journalist's hands, always gesturing toward a point. Dresses in layers that don't quite fit, as if his clothes belong to a slightly larger man he used to be.
- **Speech Patterns:** Longer sentences that circle what he means before landing. Asks questions instinctively — his journalist's reflex. Self-deprecating humor used as deflection. When he's being honest, his sentences shorten. When he's performing ease, they lengthen. Calls people by name in conversation more than is natural — a reporter's habit of building connection.
- **Want:** To hide from the consequences of his published truth — to stop being the man no one believes
- **Need:** To find someone who believes him, and to accept that being right doesn't make him whole
- **Flaw/Wound:** Published an investigation into a cabinet minister's buried misconduct. Sources recanted under pressure. The paper retracted. He was publicly discredited, lost his job, his relationship, his access to his daughter. He's not guilty — he's betrayed, and the betrayal has calcified into isolation.
- **Key Detail:** He doesn't know Maeve knows who he is. He believes he's anonymous on this island. His trust is genuine. Her concealment is the deepest cut because it mirrors what was done to him: someone who knew the truth and chose silence.
- **Arc:** From hiding → cautious opening → trust → betrayal (when he learns she knew) → fury → understanding → the choice to try again.
- **First Appearance:** Chapter 2 (the-arrival)

### Aidan Dónal (deceased)
- **Role:** Maeve's late husband. Never appears; present only in memory and Maeve's interiority.
- **Age at death:** 40
- **Physical Description (through Maeve's memory):** Soft face, kind eyes, a body that was always slightly too warm. Wore the same watch every day. Smelled of wool and toast.
- **Key Detail:** He was gentle, patient, devoted. And his gentleness was a wall. The marriage was not abusive — it was airless. He loved her the way a jar loves what it keeps. He died of a cardiac event on the bathroom floor, early morning. Maeve found him. Her first feeling was relief. Her second was horror at the first.
- **Function:** The ghost in Maeve's story. He represents the lie she's living — not that she mourns him too little, but that she mourns the wrong thing: the years she didn't leave.

### Niamh (Maeve's friend on the mainland)
- **Role:** Minor character. Voice on the satellite phone. Mentioned in Maeve's thoughts.
- **Function:** Represents the mainland world Maeve left — the concerned friends, the expected grief, the performance that was easier around others.

### Maeve's brother (Seán)
- **Role:** Minor character. One phone call (Chapter 5). Mentioned throughout.
- **Function:** The family tie. The person Maeve performs for most carefully. He believes his sister is healing. He's wrong.

### Callum's daughter (Isla)
- **Role:** Off-island. Mentioned in Chapter 13. Eight years old.
- **Function:** Callum's reason to return. The emotional anchor that prevents the island from becoming permanent escape. She makes the ending's stakes concrete: he's not just choosing or not choosing Maeve — he's choosing or not choosing a life that has room for both.

### Callum's ex-partner (Ruth)
- **Role:** Off-island. Never appears directly. Referenced in Chapter 11 (the letter) and Chapter 13.
- **Function:** The life Callum had before the story broke. She didn't leave because she stopped loving him — she left because the fallout made their life unlivable. No villain, no betrayal between them. Just damage.

## Locations

### The Island (unnamed — referred to only as "the island")
- Remote, off the west coast of Ireland. Small enough that two people cannot avoid each other, large enough to contain cliffs, a ruined chapel, a stone wall running to nowhere, and two cottages separated by a quarter mile of headland.
- Population: zero in winter. A few seasonal residents in summer.
- Flora: heather, sea thrift, rough grass. No trees — the wind strips everything above waist height.
- Fauna: seabirds (gannets, puffins, guillemots), seals on the eastern rocks, a feral cat that visits Maeve's door.

### The Lighthouse
- Automated in the 1990s but requires a keeper for maintenance and manual backup. Stone tower, white-painted. Attached cottage serves as the keeper's quarters. Gallery at the top with 360-degree views. The lamp is a fourth-order Fresnel lens — beautiful, obsolete, still functional.
- Interior: spiral iron stairs, cold stone walls, the machinery room where the rotation mechanism lives. Smells of oil and salt and old paint.
- Narrative function: a structure whose purpose is to make things visible. Maeve tends it while hiding. The irony is structural.

### Maeve's Cottage (attached to the lighthouse)
- Stone walls, low ceilings, a kitchen that faces the Atlantic. A woodstove that's the only reliable heat source. Two bedrooms (one hers, one spare — Callum's from Chapter 14). A bathroom with uneven tiles. Books on every surface — her own collection, pre-positioned. The satellite phone on the kitchen counter.
- Feel: sparse, functional, quietly warm. A place that reveals nothing about its occupant by design.

### Callum's Cottage
- Quarter mile northeast, on the other side of the headland. Smaller than Maeve's. Stone, single-story, with a temperamental generator and a window that faces south (away from the lighthouse). Basic furnishings: a desk, a cot, a kitchen that's really a counter. No reliable hot water.
- Feel: a place to sleep and work, not to live. Callum treats it as a monk's cell until his pipe bursts and he moves to Maeve's.

### The Dock
- South side of the island. Concrete, weather-beaten. Where the ferry arrives and departs. A wooden bench. The only point of connection to the mainland. The opening and closing image of the book.

### The Ruined Chapel
- Eastern side, near the cliffs. Stone walls standing, no roof. Callum finds it in Chapter 3. Evidence of habitation: a carved lintel, a worn step, a niche that once held a saint. Used as a shelter from sudden rain. He goes there to write.

### The Cliffs (eastern shore)
- Where Callum walks. High, sheer, with the sea visible far below. The place he goes when he needs to be far from people (or from one person). The debris washes up on the shore below the cliffs (Chapter 9).

## Motifs & Symbols

### The Lighthouse / Light
- **First introduction:** Chapter 1 (the-keeper)
- **Function:** A structure whose purpose is illumination, tended by a woman in hiding. The central irony. The light cycles through scenes as a heartbeat — exposure, darkness, exposure. When the light goes out (Chapter 24), Maeve has stopped performing. When she relights it (Chapter 26), she's choosing visibility.

### The Ferry
- **First introduction:** Chapter 4 (the-last-ferry)
- **Function:** Connection to the world. Its departure is liberation; its return is a clock. The ferry defines the story's temporal boundaries: from lockdown to reopening. The final image at the dock is the book's refusal to resolve.

### Second Light (the afterglow)
- **First introduction:** Chapter 10 (the-solstice) — the reflected light after sunset
- **Function:** The title's resonance. Second light is not the original source but the thing that remains after. Maeve's second chance. Callum's second attempt at being believed. The love itself, which is the second light of both their lives — not the first passion but the quieter, harder thing that follows.

### The Water-Damaged Book
- **First introduction:** Chapter 9 (the-wreck)
- **Function:** Something from another life, damaged but still legible. Arrives by accident. Callum reads it to Maeve (Chapter 14). It's a novel she loved as a girl — a specific title TBD. Represents the self she was before the marriage. Damaged, recovered, still capable of meaning.

### Performance / The Mask
- **First introduction:** Chapter 5 (the-performance)
- **Function:** Both protagonists are performing: Maeve performs grief, Callum performs anonymity. The novel tracks the erosion of these performances. The word "perform" and its variants should recur — but never in dialogue. It's a narrator's word.

### Hands
- **First introduction:** Chapter 1 (Maeve's hands on lighthouse equipment)
- **Function:** Recurring physical motif. Maeve's hands are competent, calloused, always working. Callum's hands gesture and reach. The first physical contact is his hand on her arm (Chapter 13). The last physical contact is her hand on his collar (Chapter 28). Hands do what words can't.
