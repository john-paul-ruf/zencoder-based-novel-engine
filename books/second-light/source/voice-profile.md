# Voice Profile — Second Light

> Completed by Verity. Calibrated for upmarket book club fiction with literary romance core.

## Known from Pitch
- **Tone:** Restrained, atmospheric, emotionally precise. Quiet tension beneath surface stillness. Literary weight with commercial accessibility.
- **Comp Titles for Tone:** *Normal People* (Rooney) for emotional restraint and charged silences; *The Light Between Oceans* (Stedman) for atmosphere and moral gravity; *The Storied Life of A.J. Fikry* (Zevin) for warmth and book club resonance.
- **POV & Tense:** Dual third-person limited, past tense
- **Audience Register:** Adult — readers who want emotional depth without pretension. Book club and BookTok crossover. Comfortable with ambiguity in endings.

## Craft Dimensions

- **Sentence Rhythm:** Medium sentences as the baseline — muscular, clean, unburdened. Short fragments for emotional punctuation: moments of realization, shifts in power, physical sensation. Longer sentences reserved for interiority and landscape, where the prose breathes with the setting. Rhythm mirrors the Atlantic: long swells broken by sharp crests.

- **Vocabulary Register:** Mid-literary. Precise but never showy. Prefers the right concrete noun over the impressive abstract one. "Salt" over "salinity." "Wind" over "gale force." Allows herself one striking image per scene — a single metaphor that earns its keep — and lets the rest stay clean. No vocabulary that would make a reader reach for a dictionary or feel talked down to.

- **Dialogue Style:** Naturalistic and spare. Characters talk around what they mean. Subtext does the heavy lifting — what's unsaid matters more than what's spoken. Dialogue tags are minimal: "said" and action beats. Characters interrupt, trail off, change the subject. Silence is a line of dialogue. Maeve speaks in short, deflecting sentences. Callum speaks in longer ones that circle what he actually wants to say.

- **Emotional Temperature:** Warm underneath, cool on the surface. Sentiment is earned through accumulation — small physical details, repeated gestures, the weight of routine. The prose never announces emotion; it builds pressure until the reader feels it before the character names it. Tenderness arrives sideways, in a handed cup or a door left open.

- **Interiority Depth:** Deep but disciplined. Third-person limited sits close to each POV character's consciousness — we hear their thoughts, but filtered through the narrator's restraint. Interiority reveals what the character is lying to themselves about, not what the reader already knows. Maeve's sections are denser internally (she's hiding more). Callum's are more observational (he's a journalist — he notices the world before he notices himself).

- **Punctuation Habits:** Em-dashes for interruption and pivot — both in dialogue and narration. Occasional comma splice for momentum when two thoughts press against each other. Sparing use of semicolons; they appear in reflective passages but never in dialogue. No exclamation marks in narration. Ellipses only in dialogue, for trailing off.

- **Structural Instincts:** Chapters are short (2,500–3,500 words). Scenes begin late and end early. White space between scenes within chapters. Alternating POV by chapter (Maeve odd, Callum even) with a few shared-space chapters in Part Three where POV becomes the reveal mechanism. Time moves in small jumps — days blur, then a single afternoon expands to fill a chapter.

- **Tonal Anchors:** Melancholic warmth. The ache of wanting something you're not sure you deserve. Rooney's emotional knife-work softened by Stedman's landscape and Zevin's faith that broken people can be gentle with each other. The humor is dry and infrequent — it surfaces in domestic absurdity (a broken generator, a disastrous attempt at bread) and disappears before it becomes a pattern.

- **Avoid:** Adverb stacking. Passive construction (unless deliberately marking a character's passivity). "Suddenly." Overwrought metaphor. Any sentence that explains what the previous sentence already showed. Dialogue tags beyond "said" and "asked." Purple descriptions of weather unless weather is doing narrative work. Sentimentality — the prose earns feeling through specificity, not declaration.
