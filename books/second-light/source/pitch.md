# Pitch Card — Second Light

> Approved 2026-03-19. Scaffolded by Spark.

## Overview

*Second Light* is an upmarket book club novel with a literary romance core, set on a remote Irish island where two strangers — a recently widowed lighthouse keeper and a disgraced journalist — are stranded together for a winter when the ferry stops running. Maeve took the lighthouse posting not to grieve but to disappear; her husband's death was a relief she cannot admit, and the performance of widowhood is the only identity she has left. Callum arrived to vanish for different reasons — he published a true story about a powerful figure, was publicly destroyed for it, and lost everything. Neither wants to be found. Both need to be. The novel tracks their slow, reluctant intimacy across the winter months, charged by a critical asymmetry: Maeve recognized Callum the moment he arrived. She knows his story before he tells it. He doesn't know she knows. Every gesture of trust is shadowed by this imbalance, and the book drives toward the unbearable question of whether honesty built on a foundation of concealment can survive the spring.

The novel positions itself between Sally Rooney's emotional precision and M.L. Stedman's atmospheric storytelling, with the broad commercial hook of forced proximity and slow-burn romance grounded by literary weight. The engine — two people whose relationship to truth is fundamentally broken, learning to be honest in a place where there's no one else to perform for — differentiates it from the crowded forced-proximity lane. The dual secrets (her relief, his rejected truth, her knowledge of his identity) provide structural tension that sustains the narrative across 75,000–85,000 words, while the open ending — the ferry returns, they stand at the dock, the reader decides — transforms the book from a love story into a meditation on whether ambiguity is a failure of love or the purest form of it. BookTok and book club audiences will carry this if the voice lands.

## Protagonist

### Maeve (working name)
- **Role:** Co-protagonist
- **Want:** To disappear — to live without being seen or known
- **Need:** To be seen fully and forgiven, starting with forgiving herself
- **Flaw/Wound:** Her marriage was something she needed to escape; her husband's death was a relief, and that truth is eating her alive beneath a performance of widowhood

### Callum (working name)
- **Role:** Co-protagonist
- **Want:** To hide from the consequences of a story the world rejected — to stop being the man no one believes
- **Need:** To find someone who believes him — and to accept that being right doesn't make him whole
- **Flaw/Wound:** He told the truth about a powerful figure, was publicly discredited, and lost everything. He's not guilty — he's betrayed, and the betrayal has calcified into isolation

## Conflict

- **External:** A remote island, a winter with no ferry, two strangers who cannot avoid each other. The physical world forces proximity; the emotional world resists it.
- **Internal:** Maeve is performing grief she doesn't feel. Callum is performing anonymity he can't sustain. She knows who he is. He doesn't know she knows. Every step toward intimacy is built on asymmetric information.
- **Engine:** The slow erosion of performance. Each act of daily life — cooking, repairing, weathering storms, silence — strips away a layer. The question isn't whether the truth comes out. It's whether what they've built can survive it.

## Emotional Arc

- **Opens with:** Stillness. Two people who have chosen disappearance, meeting in a place designed for it.
- **Drives toward:** The unbearable weight of being known — the terror and relief of someone seeing past the mask.
- **Ends with:** The ferry returns. They stand at the dock. The reader decides. What lingers is the question: is ambiguity a failure of love, or the purest form of it?

## Risk Factors

- Two-hander on an island risks pacing stagnation if subplot architecture is thin. Needs structural variety (storms, a visiting local, letters/artifacts from the past, the lighthouse itself as a narrative device).
- Open ending will polarize. Must be earned through 300 pages of emotional precision — if readers feel cheated, the book fails.
- "Forced proximity romance" is a crowded lane. Differentiation lives in the dual secrets, the power imbalance of her knowledge, and the literary register. If the voice isn't distinctive, it blends into the category.
