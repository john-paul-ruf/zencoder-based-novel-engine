# The Keeper

The light turned once every fifteen seconds. Maeve knew this the way she knew her own breathing — not because she counted, but because the rhythm had entered her body, settled into her joints, replaced whatever had been there before. Three weeks on the island and she was already becoming the thing she tended. There were worse fates.

She stood on the gallery walkway with her hands on the iron railing, the wind pulling at the knot of hair at her neck. Below, the Atlantic folded against the rocks with a patience that bordered on threat. The sky was the color of old pewter — not grey, not silver, but something between the two that had no word for itself and didn't need one.

The lighthouse was a fourth-order Fresnel. A beautiful machine. The lens rotated inside its brass housing like a slow, deliberate eye, throwing a beam that could reach fourteen nautical miles on a clear night. Most nights were not clear. Maeve preferred those — the beam cutting into fog, illuminating nothing, doing its work anyway. There was comfort in a purpose that didn't require results.

She climbed down from the gallery, the iron stairs ringing under her boots, and crossed through the machinery room where the rotation mechanism hummed its low, toothless song. The cottage was attached to the tower by a stone passageway — a throat of whitewashed wall and uneven flagstone that funneled the cold. She'd learned to walk it quickly.

The kitchen was warm. She kept the woodstove fed with a discipline that surprised her, given how little discipline she brought to anything else these days. The counter held her satellite phone, charged and silent. Her brother's number was taped to the wall beside it in her own handwriting, as if she might forget. She would not forget. She would also not call.

Maeve put the kettle on and stood with her back to the stove while it heated. The kitchen window faced west, which meant it faced the sea, which meant it faced nothing at all — just the long, grey reach of water that separated this island from every other place she'd ever lived. She'd been told, during the interview, that the posting could be isolating. She'd nodded with the right amount of serious concern and thought: *good.*

The cottage was small enough to know completely in a day. Two bedrooms, the second one stripped to a mattress and a bare bulb. A bathroom where the tiles didn't meet the wall evenly and the hot water arrived as a suggestion. The kitchen, which was also the sitting room, which was also the only room she used. Her books were on every flat surface — she'd brought two boxes and they'd colonized the space within a week, spreading across windowsills and chair arms with the quiet aggression of ivy.

She poured the tea and sat at the table. The mug was white, chipped at the rim, and she drank from the unchipped side with a care that was habit now rather than choice. Her wedding ring caught the light when she lifted the mug — a plain band, gold, that she turned with her thumb in idle moments the way other people click pens. She did not look at it. She had not looked at it in weeks. It stayed on her finger because removing it would be a decision, and decisions required a version of herself she wasn't currently running.

Outside, the wind shifted and the cottage groaned. It was a living sound, almost conversational — the stone and timber talking to the weather in a language she was beginning to understand. Three weeks and she could read the wind by the pitch of the walls. A low moan meant southwest, off the Atlantic, bringing rain. A higher, thinner sound meant north, off the open water, bringing cold. Tonight it was the low moan. She'd need to check the storm shutters on the gallery before bed.

She finished her tea and washed the mug and left it upside down on the draining board. One mug. One plate at dinner. One set of everything. The arithmetic of solitude, which she performed not with sadness but with the grim satisfaction of a problem solved. She had wanted to be one, and now she was.

The phone buzzed.

Not a call — a message, relayed through the satellite connection in bursts that arrived hours after they were sent. She picked it up. Declan, the supply coordinator on the mainland:

*Boat Tuesday. Stores as usual. Also a tenant for the Brennan cottage — writer, booked through March. Name is Greig. He's been told about the generator. Don't worry about showing him anything beyond the basics.*

Maeve read it twice. She set the phone down and pressed her palms flat on the counter.

She had not been told there would be someone else.

That wasn't entirely true. She'd been told, technically, that the Brennan cottage — the other habitable building on the island, a quarter mile northeast, on the far side of the headland — was occasionally let to artists and writers seeking solitude. She'd been told this in the way people tell you about the emergency exits on a plane: as a formality that would never apply. The cottage hadn't been let in two years. The island was not a destination. It was a place people ended up when every other option had been exhausted.

She knew this because it was why she was here.

Maeve turned the ring on her finger, three full rotations, and stopped. The lighthouse beam swept past the kitchen window, its light filling the room for one bright second before moving on. In the after-dark, the kitchen felt smaller. She thought about the word "tenant" and what it implied: someone who would be here, on her island, for five months. Someone she would have to see and speak to and be near. Someone who would require a version of herself she was not currently offering anyone.

She thought about calling Declan and saying it was unacceptable, she'd taken this posting for the isolation, the contract was clear. But the contract wasn't clear — she'd read it, and it said nothing about exclusive occupancy. She'd assumed. Assumption was not a legal argument.

She climbed the stairs and stood on the gallery in the wind. The beam turned. Fifteen seconds of darkness. One second of light. Fifteen seconds of darkness. One second of light. The ratio was not lost on her.

Tuesday. Four days.

She'd need to check the generator at the Brennan cottage, make sure the gas line wasn't corroded, test the water. She'd need to find the spare key and walk the path and open the shutters and air the mattress. She'd need to be functional and competent and not the woman who sat in a dark kitchen counting the beats of a lighthouse she was paid to tend.

She could do this. She could be the keeper, and the keeper was all anyone needed her to be.

The cat appeared at the base of the tower. It came and went on a schedule of its own — a rangy ginger tom that Maeve had not named because naming implied ownership, and she was not in the business of owning things. It sat on the wet stone and looked up at her with an expression of comprehensive indifference. She went inside and put a saucer of milk by the kitchen door and did not watch it drink.

---

Tuesday came with low cloud and a sea that looked like hammered tin.

Maeve stood on the dock at nine and watched the supply boat appear around the headland. It was the *Bríd Máire*, a forty-foot trawler repurposed for cargo runs, piloted by Tadhg, who was sixty and silent and delivered stores with the emotional range of a forklift. She liked Tadhg. He asked nothing.

The boat docked and Tadhg handed up the crates — tinned food, lamp oil, batteries, two fresh loaves that would last three days before going hard. The usual. Maeve stacked them on the dock trolley and waited.

A man climbed out of the cabin.

He was medium height, broad-shouldered in a way that seemed recently diminished, as if the frame had been built for a body that was no longer filling it. Sandy hair, going grey at the temples. Wire-framed glasses he pushed up his nose as he stepped onto the dock, adjusting to the sway of solid ground. He carried one bag — a canvas holdall, the kind sold at airport shops, packed without strategy. His jacket was too thin for the island. He'd learn.

He looked at the lighthouse, then at the sky, then at her.

"Maeve?" he said.

She heard the accent — Scottish, Edinburgh, educated. A voice trained to make other people comfortable. She felt, in the half-second before she answered, something drop in her chest like a coin into deep water.

She knew this man.

Not personally. Not from any shared history or common ground. She knew him the way you know someone whose face has been in every newspaper, whose name has been spoken on every news programme, whose story has been the subject of opinion columns and podcast episodes and furious arguments in comments sections that she read at three in the morning in a house where her husband slept and she could not.

Callum Greig. The journalist. The one who published the investigation into Minister Harlan and the buried inquiry. The one whose sources recanted. The one the paper called "regrettable." The one who was right, and was destroyed for being right, and disappeared.

He was standing on her dock with one bag and a thin jacket and no idea that she knew any of this.

"That's me," Maeve said. Her voice held. She was good at this — the steady tone, the level gaze, the face that offered nothing it hadn't been asked for. She'd been practising for two years. She could manage one more performance. "The cottage is up the path. I'll show you the generator."

He nodded and picked up his bag. "Thanks. I was told it could be temperamental."

"It can."

"Is there a trick to it?"

"Patience," she said. "And lower expectations."

He almost smiled. She turned and walked up the path and did not look back, and her heart was hammering, and the lighthouse turned behind them both, casting its light over the water where the boat was already retreating and the distance between this island and the rest of the world was widening with every swell.
